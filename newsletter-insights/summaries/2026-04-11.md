## ByteByteGo

### EP210: Monolithic vs Microservices vs Serverless `Engineering`
- **QA Wolf delivers 80% test coverage in weeks** — not months or quarters. This is for both web and mobile apps with unlimited parallel test runs
- **Drata case study (80+ engineers)**: Achieved 4x more test cases in the same timeframe using AI-native testing; reduced QA cycles by 86%
- **Speed multiplier: 5x faster shipping** — direct result of reducing QA cycles from hours/days to minutes. Bottleneck removal compounds velocity
- **Human-verified bug reports** — AI finds issues, humans verify severity/legitimacy before sending to eng team. Zero flakes guarantee (no false positives wasting eng time)
- **24-hour maintenance model** — tests are maintained automatically, removing the engineer burden of updating selectors and flows as app changes
- **On-demand test creation** — teams can request new test cases without waiting for QA backlog cycles
- **Cost structure**: Replaces manual E2E testing teams (expensive, slow) with AI service that scales with app growth without linear cost increase
- **Architecture trade-offs to know**:
  - **Monolith**: Single codebase + single database = operational simplicity, but scales vertically only; deployment is all-or-nothing
  - **Microservices**: Independent services enable horizontal scaling + team parallelization, but adds operational complexity (distributed debugging, data consistency)
  - **Serverless**: Abstracts infrastructure entirely; ideal for event-driven workloads; no server management; but cold starts + vendor lock-in + harder debugging
- **System design patterns**: Weekly deep-dives on AWS services, JWT visualization, CLI vs MCP comparisons, coding agent architectures

## Jay Stansell
*1 issue this period*

### The financial blind spot that gets product managers fired `Product`
- **The blindspot**: VP of Product (10 years exp, 12-person team) could NOT articulate her product's LTV-to-CAC ratio — the #1 metric boards ask first
- **What she knew instead**: NPS was 62, activation improved 14% QoQ, team shipped 23 features last cycle. All good metrics. All irrelevant to board discussion
- **The board's first question**: When do customers pay back their acquisition cost? This is the unit economics question that determines business viability
- **Nick Chasinov's blunt take**: "If CAC > LTV, it's only a matter of time before the business closes its doors" — not decades, not years, months
- **Minimum healthy threshold: 3-to-1 LTV-to-CAC ratio** — this is the bare minimum for a viable unit. Below this, customer acquisition is destroying shareholder value
- **The QBR pattern**: Product brings activation funnels and cohort retention charts. Board wants payback period. Both leave frustrated. Nothing changes until someone gets cut in budget review
- **Why this matters**: Unit economics determine company survival. Without this, you're flying blind on whether you're building a sustainable business or subsidizing customer acquisition indefinitely
- **What to calculate**: LTV = (ARPU × margin × average customer lifetime in months). CAC = (total sales+marketing spend) / (new customers acquired). Then divide LTV/CAC
- **Red flag indicator**: If product team can't answer unit economics questions, they lack visibility into what's actually driving company health
- **Strategic implication**: Even with perfect activation/NPS, if CAC > LTV, you're walking into a cost cutting crisis when growth capital runs out

### The $6 Trillion Problem of AI `AI/ML`
- **Harvey (legal AI) valuation jump**: Raised at $11B (up from $8B a year ago). That's 37% YoY valuation growth in the AI space
- **Sequoia's confidence**: Led three of Harvey's funding rounds, signaling sustained belief in the legal AI thesis
- **Harvey's revenue metrics**: Estimated $190-195M ARR (annual recurring revenue) growing 3.9x year-on-year. This is massive scale for a 4-5 year old company
- **The new SaaS thesis**: AI companies are NOT selling tools (the $1 trillion SaaS market). They're pricing FINISHED WORK as software subscriptions (the $6 trillion professional services market)
- **Product model**: Law firms send Harvey legal research + contract review + case memos → Harvey returns completed work → billed like SaaS, not hourly labor
- **Economic reframe example**: QuickBooks costs law firm $10,000/year. The accountant using QuickBooks costs $120,000/year. New AI companies want to sell the $120k outcome directly
- **Mercor case study**: Building marketplace for AI agents as freelancers — same play: finished work (coding, engineering tasks) priced as SaaS output, not human-hour consulting
- **Market size implication**: $1T SaaS market fought over software licensing. $6T professional services market (legal, accounting, consulting, engineering) is being directly automated and re-sold as software
- **Valuation logic**: Harvey's $11B isn't for legal software tools. It's for an engine that replaces $X billion of legal labor with AI and captures margins by selling output as software
- **Sequoia's framing**: Two decades of software fought over the $1. The new wave goes straight for the $6
- **Why this matters for founders**: If you're building AI products, ask: am I selling software tools to humans, or finished work in a new package? The latter is a 6x bigger market
- **Sustainability question**: These companies scale because they're capturing professional services margins ($6T) not software margins ($1T) — fundamentally different unit economics
