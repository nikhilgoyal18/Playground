- Deliverable is not a product concept but an architecture diagram showing data flows, model choices, agent roles, orchestration patterns, and failure handling

- 64-question practice bank plus 2 full worked examples (search/retrieval and content moderation) included
- Aakash created a Claude Skill, Custom GPT, and Gemini Gem to help candidates practice — signals that AI-native interview prep tooling is now expected
- Guide framed as "first and only comprehensive PM AI system design interview guide on the internet" — positioning gap in a market where $1M+ roles are being filled

## Jay Stansell — Product Coalition
*1 issue this period*

### The CFO Questions Every Product Leader Must Answer About AI Agents `Product`
- 95% of GenAI pilots deliver zero ROI (Bulbul Pandya, Product Coalition): "AI doesn't absolve us from doing the product work. It magnifies the consequences of skipping it"
- AI inference cost dropped 99% in two years — but cheaper tokens mean teams use 100x more of them; some product orgs saw AI compute go from a rounding error to their third-largest line item in under a year
- SaaS pricing model shift: Serhat Pala frames "SaaS 2.0" as per-outcome vs. per-seat — "SaaS 1.0 charged per seat. Customers paid for access. SaaS 2.0 charges per outcome. Customers pay for work completed: a support ticket resolved, a tax return filed, a contract reviewed"
- Per-outcome pricing flips the entire risk model: if an agent requires human intervention 20% of the time, gross margin might be 60%; if 40% intervention rate, you're running a service business with software pricing — accuracy rate becomes a margin metric, not just a product metric
- Three CFO questions product leaders must answer: (1) "What's the fully loaded cost per agent-completed task, including compute, orchestration, evaluation, and human escalation?" (2) "What's the error rate, and what does each error cost us?" (3) "At what volume does this become cheaper than the humans, contractors, or BPO teams doing the same work today?"
- Thomas Schwenger quote on the infrastructure shift: "The translation layer between business ideas and working software has been the most expensive bottleneck in technology for decades. It is collapsing" — but the cost moved from headcount to infrastructure; CFOs want to know where it landed
- Jon Matheson cited on PM accountability: product managers who don't understand how their decisions affect revenue, COGS, and margin are doing half the job; with agents the stakes are higher because a wrong output can be a compliance event, not just a bug ticket
- Teams shipping agent features without a unit economics model — no cost-per-task target, no margin threshold, no kill switch when math stops working — are the ones getting flagged in quarterly reviews
- Key framing: "The product leaders who thrive in this era won't be the ones who ship the most agents. They'll be the ones who can show the unit economics on a single slide and defend every number on it"

## Product School
*1 issue this period*

### Zoom CPO Jeff Smith on Rebuilding for the Agentic Era `Product`
- Jeff Smith has been Zoom CPO since 2019 — joined just after IPO, just before the pandemic — and has lived through three distinct product eras; COVID forced a decade of remote work acceptance in months
- Zoom tripled headcount in roughly 12 months during the pandemic; the architecture held because of decisions made years earlier — "it was an overnight success. No, it was 10 years in the making"
- Reliability and quality as compounding strategic investments: teams that build above market expectations while demand is low are the ones that don't break when demand spikes
- Multi-product expansion must follow the user's job lifecycle, not the revenue map: Zoom mapped the full lifecycle of why someone has a meeting (preparation, execution, follow-through) and built surfaces for every stage — Calendar, Mail, Whiteboard, and async coordination are not diversification bets but answers to "what does someone need before and after a meeting to accomplish something?"
- Coexistence is a real strategy against entrenched tools (Google, Microsoft, Salesforce): "I've got to offer something that has an order of magnitude higher value in order to overcome those switching costs" — Zoom's answer is to integrate and expose capabilities via MCP into third-party surfaces, not demand displacement
- When engineering velocity becomes abundant, strategy becomes the bottleneck: "The challenge becomes that [it] pushes upstream the need to — am I doing the right stuff? Is the strategic direction, the outcome-driven decision making, that becomes so much more important"
- Zoom restructured its spec process entirely: stakeholders are brought in earlier and AI Companion drafts the spec from that meeting in real time — a direct operational response to AI-accelerated shipping outpacing strategic clarity
- MCP and the agentic layer as non-optional: Zoom plays both sides — building AI capabilities within its own surface AND exposing them via MCP for third-party environments; Jeff's reasoning: "We don't know how this is going to evolve. And so what we need to do is we have to play in each one of these spaces"
- Engagement depth vs. frequency as the right AI metric: auto-summarizing every meeting is high frequency; generating a board-ready presentation from months of meeting transcripts, Salesforce signals, and deep research outputs is high value — Jeff tracks the distance between those two states as an engagement funnel
- Aligned incentives framing: "I want deeper engagement because it makes people more retained in our platform, but they're only retained if they're getting more value. So that's the — I like that where it's not something that I'm extracting. It's a partnership between us and our users"
- Practical advice: companies that close themselves off to the agentic layer will be unable to serve users who live across multiple ecosystems; don't wait for a perfect strategy — start experimenting now to avoid calibrating from scratch when the layer matures

### A Guide to Relational Database Design `Engineering`
- The hardest part of relational database design is not SQL syntax but the thinking that precedes any code: which pieces of information deserve their own table, how tables should reference each other, and how much redundancy is acceptable
- Three core design questions frame every schema decision: (1) which entities warrant their own table, (2) how tables reference each other via keys, (3) the acceptable threshold for data redundancy
- Getting these design decisions wrong means spending months patching problems baked into the structure from day one — schema mistakes compound and become structurally painful to reverse
- The guide builds concepts sequentially: tables → keys → relationships → normalization → joins; each concept builds on the last rather than treating them as isolated topics
- Normalization is positioned as the tool for resolving redundancy and consistency trade-offs — the mechanism that answers "how much duplication is too much"
- Joins are framed as the output layer of good relational design — when table structure and keys are designed correctly, joins stay fast and queries stay maintainable
- The article is part of ByteByteGo's pattern of turning foundational engineering concepts into structured decision frameworks rather than syntax tutorials — aimed at the design-thinking gap between knowing SQL and knowing how to model data
