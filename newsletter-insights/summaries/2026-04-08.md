### How Spotify Ships to 675 Million Users Every Week Without Breaking Things `Engineering`
- Release cycle timing: "Every Friday morning, a team at Spotify takes hundreds of code changes written by dozens of engineering teams and begins packaging them into a single app update" — reaching 675M users across Android, iOS, and Desktop
- Platform distribution: 95%+ of releases ship to every user without breaking, achieved through architectural design choices, not testing hardness
- Development pattern: trunk-based development (all code merges to main branch immediately after review) with no long-lived feature branches — keeps integration problems small but requires discipline
- Weekly cadence: version number gets bumped on Friday; nightly builds start going out to Spotify employees and external alpha testers during the first week
- Feedback loop: crash rates and other quality metrics tracked both automatically and by human review; bug reports flow in from internal and alpha users
- Severity thresholds: when a crash or issue crosses predefined severity threshold, escalation is triggered
- Speed reinforces safety: speed and safety reinforce each other in this architecture—the design trades complexity for release reliability

### Nextdoor's Database Evolution: A Scaling Ladder `Engineering`
- Platform model: "Nextdoor operates as a hyper-local social networking service that connects neighbors based on their geographic location" — data must be highly available and extremely accurate
- Scaling constraint: early single PostgreSQL instance hit connection limits not due to total data size, but the process-per-connection model where "every time an application worker wants to talk to the database, the server creates a completely new process"
- Architecture progression from single instance: connection poolers → read replicas → versioned caches → background reconcilers
- Fundamental scaling principle: "Every performance gain introduces a new requirement for data integrity"
- Distributed system tradeoff: each scaling step trades simplicity for availability; teams moved from simple setup into sophisticated distributed architecture
- Integrity enforcement: background reconcilers ensure data consistency across the layered system as complexity grows
- Reference: based on publicly shared details from Nextdoor Engineering Team

### The North Star That Wasn't `Product`
- 2019 consensus: Smit Shah's "One Metric that Matters (OMTM)" from September 2019 advocated "The concept of One Metric that Matters (OMTM) states that any organization should focus on optimizing only one metric that matters at that particular stage of the product's lifecycle"
- Evangelism peak: "The certainty in it is wild" — pick one number, align the team, ship toward that number; metric should shift as product matures but the core belief was unwavering
- First crack: Six months later (early 2020), Rashmi Shukla wrote "Product Metrics — How to Measure a Product" arguing for "chasing a north star metric accompanied by a few major metrics" (emphasis: "a few", not one)
- Vanity metric warning: Rashmi warned focusing on MAU is "just another vanity metric" that hides the real picture of product stickiness — a caveat that didn't get enough attention at the time
- 2021 inflection: "Two pieces from 2021 basically describe the wreckage" of teams that followed the OMTM advice correctly by the book but still ended up in trouble
- Backlash evidence: Tiziano Nessi wrote "How to Position Yourself as a Strong Product Manager From the Get-Go" (2021) describing how OMTM approach "nearly wrecked their product teams"
- Timeline arc: 2019-2020 single-metric evangelism → 2020 first warnings ("a few metrics") → 2021 backlash from early evangelists themselves
- Shift in thinking: evolution from "one metric to rule them all" to "north star accompanied by supporting metrics" — the shift happened gradually and many PMs missed it as it occurred
