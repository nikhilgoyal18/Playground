### How LinkedIn Feed Uses LLMs to Serve 1.3 Billion Users `Engineering`
- LinkedIn replaced five separate heterogeneous retrieval systems (chronological index, trending posts, collaborative filtering, industry pipelines, embedding retrieval) with a single LLM-powered dual encoder architecture — a radical simplification bet that enabled holistic optimization across the full candidate set
- The dual encoder architecture converts both members and posts into vectors in the same mathematical space using a shared LLM; nearest-neighbor search retrieves candidates in under 50ms for 1.3B users — the key engineering constraint that shaped all design decisions
- **Critical insight on numerical features**: Passing raw engagement counts (e.g., "views:12345") into LLM prompts produced near-zero correlation (-0.004) between popularity signals and embedding similarity — LLMs treat digit tokens as text, not magnitude; LinkedIn fixed this by converting raw counts into percentile buckets or categorical labels (e.g., "top 5% most-engaged")
- LLM-generated embeddings solved the cold-start problem: a new member with only a profile headline can have interests inferred from world knowledge baked into the LLM's pretraining, without waiting for engagement history to accumulate
- The "prompt library" approach: structured profile features (skills, work history, engagement sequence) and post features (author info, engagement counts, post text) are converted into templated text before being fed to the LLM — essentially prompt engineering applied to recommendation systems at scale
- Downstream ranking improvement was a compounding benefit: when all candidates come from a single semantically coherent source (vs. five systems with different biases), every optimization to the ranking model becomes more effective — unified retrieval makes the whole stack easier to tune
- Key lesson for ML engineers: the hardest part of LLM-powered recommendation isn't the model — it's the feature representation problem; structured data must be thoughtfully translated into text that preserves signal fidelity (especially numerical magnitude)

## PM Diego's Newsletter

### Your PM Team Needs an AI Operating System `Product`
- Guest author Ayushman Jain (Microsoft, 10 years at MSFT and Amazon, built Microsoft's first AI agents) identifies the core problem: individual PMs build personal AI workflows that don't compound — "ten separate prompt libraries, ten separate repositories… zero compounding value across the team"
- Jensen Huang framing: a $500K engineer should be consuming at least $250K of AI compute; for a $400-600K fully loaded senior PM spending 80% of their week on zero-lasting-value work (status updates, QBR prep, PRD iteration), AI hasn't fixed the structural problem — "most AI tools make the 80% slightly faster"
- The architectural distinction: **chat AI** (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) resets every session with no memory; **agentic workspace** (a structured folder with a CLAUDE.md config file containing product OKRs, customer history, team principles) starts every session grounded in actual context automatically
- Concrete workflow example: PM logs a customer call Tuesday (objection, template ask, renewal stall); teammate opening the workspace Thursday gets a structured brief from Tuesday's notes before joining the same customer's call — no Slack interruption, no dropped context
- The shared repository model (GitHub, SharePoint, Google Drive) converts individual PM discipline into institutional knowledge: every customer call, PRD, and decision logged by any team member becomes available to the whole team on next workspace open
- Open-sourced PM Workspace template available (GitHub link referenced); core structure is a CLAUDE.md config + organized subfolders for competitive intelligence, customer notes, OKRs, and working principles — designed to work with Claude Code or Cowork as the agentic runtime
- Implication for product orgs: The bottleneck isn't AI capability — it's AI infrastructure (shared, persistent context); teams that solve this compound knowledge while teams with individual setups stay flat

## Product Coalition (Jay Stansell)

### Executive Performance Score: Collapsing 8 Metrics Into 1 for Voice Coaching `Product`
- ExecReps built acoustic voice analysis measuring pace, filler words, clarity, and confidence across 4 delivery dimensions (Command, Eloquence, Engagement, Consistency) plus GPT-4 content analysis across 4 dimensions — but watching themselves use their own dashboard revealed Don Norman's "Gulf of Evaluation" problem: more data, zero answers
- Solution: The Executive Performance Score (EPS), a single 400-1000 scale number combining content quality (GPT-4 structural/persuasion/clarity analysis) and delivery quality (acoustic analysis) — mental model shifts from "8 metrics to track" to "one number that goes up when I get better"
- **Weighting decision (60/40 content/delivery vs. 50/50)**: The 50/50 split was rejected because it created a perverse incentive — people with strong voices but weak content would score "good" when they were just pleasant-sounding; academic communication research supports content predicting perceived competence more strongly than delivery alone in professional contexts (board meetings, pitches)
- Fitbit comparison is the design principle: raw biometric data (VO2 max, HRV) is table-stakes; the "body battery" synthesis is the product delighter — same sensors, but the experience shifts from "here's your data" to "here's your answer"; same principle applied to voice coaching
- Kano Model framing: the raw metrics are expected quality (table-stakes); the EPS composite is the delighter — and the raw data lives "behind a tap for nerds who want it," not front-and-center
- Lesson for product builders: data is not the product; the interpretation is the product; collecting more dimensions without providing synthesis creates the paradox of choice (Schwartz), not insight

### Behavioral Science of Retention: Streaks Without the Hostage-Taking `Product`
- ExecReps had an activation cliff: users either fell off in the first week or stayed for months; Hook Model audit scored them 4/10 — good trigger (email invite) and action (workout), but flat Variable Reward and no Investment phase to close the loop
- Duolingo's streak system identified as the anti-pattern: users maintaining 300-day streaks without learning new words — metric completely decoupled from outcome; Kano "delighter" degraded into a "must-be" obligation that only generates dissatisfaction when broken ("a hostage situation")
- BJ Fogg's B=MAP reframe: the question isn't "how do we motivate people to practice?" — it's "how do we make practicing the path of least resistance?"; design for exhausted Thursday afternoon, not eager Monday morning
- **Four behavioral frameworks applied simultaneously**:
  - BJ Fogg B=MAP: design for low-motivation moment, not peak motivation
  - Kahneman & Tversky Prospect Theory: losses felt 2x more than gains — use loss aversion as a feature, not a bug
  - Nir Eyal Hook Model: Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment; the missing Investment phase was closing the loop for re-engagement
  - Deci & Ryan Self-Determination Theory / Pink's AMP (Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose): engagement systems that undermine autonomy eventually backfire
- **Streak freeze mechanic**: miss a day, spend a "freeze credit" banked through consistent practice — converts loss aversion into a reward for past behavior (Cialdini's Commitment & Consistency principle); you're spending something you earned, not being punished
- Streak break UX: instead of a sad owl, show "Your best: 23 days. Let's start a new one." — reframes failure as a personal record benchmark, preserves motivation to restart
- Celebration timing matters: positive feedback must fire within 200ms of completion to wire the habit (Fogg's "celebration" mechanism); delayed celebration doesn't anchor the behavior
